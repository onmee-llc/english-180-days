## Session goal

Write and say a decline message that is warm, direct, and decisive.

## Shadowing passage

> Declining an offer is a conversation that most engineers handle awkwardly because they either delay too long or over-explain. Here is the approach I use. I always decline by phone or video, not email, if the process has been personal. And I do it quickly — within 24 hours of making my decision. 'Thank you so much for the offer and for the time you invested in the interview process. I have given this serious consideration, and I have decided to go in a different direction. This was not an easy decision — the team and the problem are genuinely interesting. The offer I accepted is a closer fit to the specific technical direction I am pursuing at this stage of my career. I want to be respectful of your time by letting you know as soon as possible. I hope our paths cross again — I have followed the company's work for a while and I have a lot of respect for what you are building.' Then stop. Do not give details about the other offer. Do not invite a counter. You have made your decision. Be decisive and kind.

| closer fit | /ˈkloʊzər fɪt/ | neutral reason — doesn't imply the declined offer is inferior |
| be decisive and kind | /dɪˈsaɪsɪv ænd kaɪnd/ | the two traits that make a professional decline |

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read the passage silently twice. Focus on meaning, not pronunciation.

**Minutes 6–15** — Shadow aloud with the text visible. 4 full passes. Match rhythm and stress.

**Minutes 16–22** — Shadow without the text. 3 passes. Accept mistakes — keep moving.

**Minutes 23–28** — Record yourself once. Play it back immediately. Note one phrase to improve.

**Minutes 29–30** — Write the phrase you struggled with. Say it 10 times slowly, then at full speed.

## Reflection

Have you ever delayed declining an offer? What is the actual cost of that delay to the other party?
