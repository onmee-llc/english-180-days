## 1. Stating Your Expectation Clearly

**When asked:** "What is your salary expectation?"

**The approach:**
- Anchor on your value first, then state the number
- Give a range, not a single number
- The bottom of your range = the number you'd actually accept
- Immediately ask about the full package

**Example:**
> "Based on my twelve years of experience in backend engineering and AI integration, and the scope of this role, I'm targeting five thousand to six thousand dollars per month. I'm open to discussing the full compensation package. What does the compensation structure for this role look like?"

**Never say:** "I'm flexible." "Whatever you think is fair." "What's the budget?"

## 2. Responding to an Offer

**When you receive an offer:** Never accept or reject immediately. Always buy time.

**Example:**
> "Thank you for the offer — I'm genuinely interested in this role. Could I have a couple of days to review the details and get back to you?"

**Why:** Time gives you leverage. You can research, compare, and prepare your counter-offer. Immediate acceptance signals desperation.

## 3. Negotiating Upward

**The approach:** Express enthusiasm first, then make your case with evidence.

**Example:**
> "I'm very excited about this opportunity. The offer is close to what I had in mind, but based on my experience building production AI pipelines and the value I'd bring to the team from day one, I was hoping we could get to six thousand. Is there flexibility there?"

**Key principles:**
- Always anchor on the value you bring, not what you need
- Reference specific skills that are hard to find
- Use market data if available ("Based on market research for this role in this region…")
- Frame it as a conversation, not a demand

## 4. Discussing Total Compensation

**Beyond base salary:**
- **Equity / stock options** — ask about vesting schedule (typically 4-year with 1-year cliff)
- **Benefits** — health insurance, dental, vision, retirement matching
- **Remote work allowance** — equipment budget, co-working space, internet stipend
- **Learning budget** — conferences, courses, certifications
- **PTO (Paid Time Off)** — how many days, sick leave policy
- **Performance review timeline** — when is the first salary review? (push for 6 months, not 12)

**Example:**
> "Beyond the base salary, could you walk me through the full package? I'm particularly interested in understanding the equity structure, the remote work setup, and when the first performance review would be."

## 5. Closing Professionally

**Accepting:**
> "I'm excited to accept. Thank you for working with me on the package. I'm looking forward to joining the team. What are the next steps?"

**Declining:**
> "Thank you very much for the offer and the time you've invested. After careful consideration, I've decided to pursue a different opportunity that aligns more closely with my goals. I hope we might work together in the future."

**Asking for more time:**
> "I'm very interested, but I want to make a thoughtful decision. Would it be possible to have until Friday to give you a final answer?"

## The Negotiation Mindset

**Key truths:**
1. The company expects you to negotiate. Not negotiating is unusual at senior level.
2. You're not being greedy. You're establishing your professional value.
3. The hiring manager is often your advocate — they want to close you.
4. Every $500/month increase = $6,000/year. Over 3 years = $18,000. Worth 15 minutes of discomfort.
5. If they rescind an offer because you negotiated professionally, you dodged a bad company.
