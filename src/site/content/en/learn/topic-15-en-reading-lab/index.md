## What is the EN Reading Lab?

This track is about **reading technical English actively** — not passively skimming for information, but extracting vocabulary, identifying sentence patterns, and training yourself to summarize complex ideas in one clear English sentence.

**KPI 5 goal:** Build a weekly reading habit of 50+ technical articles by December 2026.

## Primary sources

1. **[ByteByteGo Blog](https://blog.bytebytego.com/)** — System design, distributed systems, LLM architecture, databases. Clear diagrams. Dense vocabulary.
2. **[Anthropic Research](https://www.anthropic.com/research)** — AI safety, Constitutional AI, interpretability, alignment. Advanced vocabulary. High-value for KPI 2.

## Secondary sources (alternates)

- [The Pragmatic Engineer](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/) — Engineering leadership and systems at scale
- [Martin Fowler Blog](https://martinfowler.com/) — Architecture patterns and software design

## The Reading Lab method

Each lesson follows a fixed three-part structure:

**Part 1 — Skim (5 minutes)**
Read only: headings, first sentence of each paragraph, diagrams, bullet lists, and the conclusion. Do not read every word. After skimming, close the article and answer: "What is this article mainly about?"

**Part 2 — Vocabulary extraction (10 minutes)**
Re-read the article. Extract 5–8 technical words or phrases you didn't know or weren't sure of. For each: write the word, its context sentence from the article, and a short definition in English.

**Part 3 — One-sentence summary (5 minutes)**
Write one sentence in English that captures the core argument or finding of the article. No more than 30 words. Example format: "[Article title] argues that [main claim] by showing [evidence/method]."

## Daily lesson structure

Each of the 14 lessons features one article from ByteByteGo or Anthropic Research with the three-part Reading Lab protocol.
