## Session goal

Describe the six-part talk structure and practice the opening and closing for your Tech Talk #1.

## Shadowing passage

> A technical talk that rambles is not a bad talk about a good topic — it's a disrespectful use of the audience's time. Every talk I've given that landed well had the same six-part structure. Open with a hook: a question, a surprising number, or a problem statement that the audience immediately recognizes as their own. Frame the problem: what pain are we solving? Why does this matter right now? Present the technical core: three points, no more. Each point with one example from real work. Live demo or walkthrough: the most memorable part — even two minutes of showing the running system beats ten slides of architecture diagrams. Lessons learned: what went wrong, what you'd do differently — this is the section that makes you credible. Close with a concrete ask: not "are there any questions?" but "here is the one thing I want you to take away from today." I practice the opening and closing most. If you nail the first 60 seconds, you have the audience's trust. If you nail the last 60 seconds, you have their action. The middle can have some rough edges — the audience forgives imperfection if the frame is clear and the ending is strong.

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read silently twice.
**Minutes 6–15** — Shadow aloud 4 times.
**Minutes 16–22** — Shadow without text. 3 passes.
**Minutes 23–28** — Write your Tech Talk #1 opening hook and closing ask. Practice them out loud.
**Minutes 29–30** — Record your opening (60 sec) and closing (30 sec).
