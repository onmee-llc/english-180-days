## Session goal

Handle 5 practice Q&A scenarios out loud — including one hostile question and one you genuinely don't know the answer to.

## Shadowing passage

> Q&A is where many technically strong engineers lose the room. They can deliver a polished talk, but when an unexpected question arrives, they freeze, go quiet, or give a disorganized answer that undermines everything that came before. There are three scenarios to prepare for. The first is the question you don't know. The wrong response is making something up or giving a long, vague non-answer. The right response: "That's an area I haven't explored in depth. My instinct would be to approach it by — " and give one sentence of reasoning. That shows intellectual honesty and analytical thinking. The second is the hostile or skeptical question. Someone challenges your approach: "Your latency is 4 seconds — that seems unacceptably slow." Don't be defensive. Acknowledge the concern: "That's a fair point. For a chat interface, 4 seconds would not be acceptable. In our use case — async document Q&A where users submit a question and check back — it's within the acceptable range. If we were building a real-time chat, we'd need a very different architecture." The third is the off-topic tangent. Someone asks a detailed question that would derail the talk. "That's an interesting question — slightly outside the scope of today, but something I'd be happy to discuss offline. Let me come back to the main thread and we can continue that conversation after."

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read silently twice.
**Minutes 6–12** — Shadow the three scenarios.
**Minutes 13–28** — Ask yourself the 5 practice questions from the Practice Questions module. Answer each one out loud. Include the hostile question from Q4.
**Minutes 29–30** — Record the hostile question answer. Listen back. Were you defensive or composed?
