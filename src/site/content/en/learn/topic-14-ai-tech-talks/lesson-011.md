## Session goal

Prepare your post-talk feedback form and practice responding to three types of feedback in English.

## Shadowing passage

> Collecting feedback after a tech talk is not optional — it's the only way to know whether the talk achieved its goal. I use a five-question form that takes two minutes to fill in. One: what was the most useful thing from this talk? Two: what was confusing or unclear? Three: what would you like to know more about? Four: on a scale of 1 to 5, how likely are you to apply something from this talk in your work? Five: anything else you'd like to share? I send it immediately after the talk while the content is fresh — anything beyond 24 hours and the response rate drops significantly. When feedback comes in, I respond to it professionally. Positive feedback: "Thank you — glad the demo was useful. Let me know if you have questions as you try to apply it." Constructive feedback: "That's a fair point about the latency discussion — I moved through it quickly. I'll add more detail to the notes I'll share with the team." Critical feedback: "I appreciate the honesty. I'll revise the slides before the recording goes out." The mistake most people make with feedback is either ignoring it or becoming defensive about it. The feedback is a gift — even when it's blunt. Engineers who can receive and act on feedback are better communicators than engineers who can only give it.

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read silently twice.
**Minutes 6–12** — Shadow the passage.
**Minutes 13–20** — Create your 5-question feedback form. Adapt the questions for your Tech Talk #1.
**Minutes 21–28** — Practice responding to three types of feedback aloud: positive, constructive, critical.
**Minutes 29–30** — Record one response to a critical piece of feedback. Is it defensive or composed?

## Reflection

Prepare three specific responses for feedback you expect after Tech Talk #1.
