## How to use this module

1. Read the outline once silently.
2. Close the page. Try to recall the main points out loud.
3. Come back and fill in what you missed.
4. Repeat until you can present the outline verbally in 3–5 minutes.

---

## 1. Talk Structure

**Opening hook** — The first 60 seconds determine whether the audience pays attention for the next 45 minutes. Open with a question, a surprising fact, or a concrete problem — not "Today I'm going to talk about…"

**Problem framing** — Before any solution, establish the problem clearly. What pain does the audience recognize? Why does it matter to them specifically?

**Technical core** — The 3–4 main points of the talk. Each point: what it is, why it matters, one real example. Avoid the temptation to cover everything — cover the three things you want people to remember.

**Live demo or walkthrough** — The most memorable part of a tech talk. Even a 5-minute code walkthrough makes abstract concepts concrete. Narrate every step aloud as if the audience can't see your screen.

**Lessons learned** — What went wrong. What you'd do differently. This is the most credible part of any talk — it shows you've shipped real things and learned from them.

**Call to action** — End with one concrete ask: try this, read this, apply this, reach out with questions. Not a vague "any questions?"

## 2. Slides

**One idea per slide** — A slide with six bullet points means you're reading from the slide. One concept per slide forces you to explain it in your own words.

**Visuals over text** — Diagrams, architecture charts, and code snippets communicate faster than prose. For LLM talks: data flow diagrams, cost graphs, latency charts.

**Speaker notes** — Write your key phrases for each slide in the speaker notes. Not a script — just the 2–3 phrases you must say for each slide.

**Version your slides** — Save a version before every practice run. After the talk, archive the final version in the team's shared folder.

## 3. Delivery

**Pacing** — Most non-native speakers talk too fast when nervous. Aim for 120–140 words per minute — slower than feels natural.

**Pausing** — Use silence intentionally. After a key statement, pause 2 seconds. The audience needs time to absorb.

**Handling unknown vocabulary** — If a technical term slips your mind mid-sentence: describe it ("it's the thing that…"), use a synonym, or skip it and come back. Never freeze.

**Eye contact** — In a room, make eye contact with 3–4 people in different areas of the room. Online: look at the camera, not the screen.

## 4. Q&A Handling

**Buying time** — "That's a great question. Let me think about that for a moment." / "Can you clarify what you mean by…?" These are professional, not stalling tactics.

**"I don't know"** — The most credible answer when you don't know. Follow with: "But I'd approach it by…" or "I'll find out and follow up."

**Hostile questions** — Acknowledge the concern without being defensive: "That's a valid concern. In my experience…" / "You're right that there's a trade-off here. Here's how I thought about it."

**Bridging back** — If a question takes the talk off-topic: "That's an interesting point — slightly outside today's focus, but I'd be happy to discuss it offline."

## 5. Tech Talk #2 Specifics — Lessons Learned Talk

**Honest retrospective** — What went wrong in KPI 1? Be specific: which component failed, why, and what you changed. Vague failure stories are unconvincing.

**Metrics** — Before and after. "Latency was 8 seconds; after the optimization, it dropped to 3.2 seconds." Concrete numbers make lessons credible.

**AI roadmap** — End with where you think the team should focus in the next 12 months. Not what you plan to do personally — what the team should prioritize collectively.
