## Session goal

Present three before-and-after comparisons from your KPI 1 project using specific numbers.

## Shadowing passage

> Production numbers make a talk credible in a way that no amount of architecture diagrams can. Before: eight seconds P95 latency. After: 3.2 seconds. Before: $800 per month in token costs. After: $180. Before: 8% context window errors. After: 0.3%. Those three sentences convey more about engineering competence than three slides of system design. But numbers without context are meaningless. For each metric, I provide three pieces of context. First, what changed: the specific engineering action that caused the improvement. Not "we optimized it" — "we added prompt length routing and reduced the P95 token count per request by 40%." Second, the timeline: how long it took to implement and how quickly the improvement appeared in production. Third, the trade-off: what we gave up for this improvement, if anything. Lower latency often costs more. Cheaper prompts sometimes reduce quality. Acknowledging trade-offs shows that you understand the whole system, not just the metric you optimized. I also distinguish between correlation and causation when presenting metrics. If latency improved the same week we changed the model, that's not necessarily because of the model change — it could be lower traffic, a different query distribution, or the provider's infrastructure improving. Presenting that correlation as causation damages your credibility.

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read silently twice.
**Minutes 6–12** — Shadow the passage.
**Minutes 13–22** — Identify three before-and-after metrics from your project. Prepare the three-part context for each.
**Minutes 23–28** — Record all three narrations.
**Minutes 29–30** — Check: is causation clear, or are you presenting correlation as causation?
