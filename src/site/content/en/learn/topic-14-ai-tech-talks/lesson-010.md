## Session goal

Present a 3-minute AI roadmap for your team — what to focus on in 2027 and why.

## Shadowing passage

> Presenting a technical roadmap is different from presenting a technical system. A system presentation is about what you built. A roadmap presentation is about what the team should build — which means you need buy-in, not just understanding. My framework for roadmap presentations has three parts. First, current state assessment: be honest about where the team is. What AI capabilities do we have today? What gaps are painful? What are the two or three things we wish we could do but can't? Second, the proposed priorities: for each priority, give the business impact first, then the technical approach. Engineers in the audience respond better to "this will reduce customer escalations by 30%" than to "we should implement semantic search." The business framing creates alignment. Third, the first step: always end with the most concrete, smallest action that starts the roadmap. Not "we should invest in AI" — "the specific thing I'm proposing we start with in Q1 is: adding an AI triage layer to the support inbox. Three sprints. Here's the architecture sketch." A roadmap presentation that ends with a clear first step is infinitely more actionable than one that ends with a vision slide.

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read silently twice.
**Minutes 6–12** — Shadow the passage.
**Minutes 13–22** — Write your team AI roadmap for 2027 using the three-part framework.
**Minutes 23–28** — Record your 3-minute roadmap presentation.
**Minutes 29–30** — Check: does it end with a concrete first step?

## Reflection

What is the one AI capability your team needs most in 2027? Prepare one paragraph in English defending that priority.

## Anti-Translation Drill
