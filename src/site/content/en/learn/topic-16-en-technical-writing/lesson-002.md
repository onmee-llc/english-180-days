## Session goal

Produce a complete article outline. The outline is the single most important planning artifact — the actual writing will be faster once it exists.

## Shadowing passage — structure before writing

> A technical article without an outline is a discovery process — you find the structure as you write. That is fine for a first draft, but it produces articles that meander. An outline forces you to answer a harder question before you start: what is the logical order of the argument? A good outline has no more than seven sections. Each section has one purpose. The introduction sets up the problem. The conclusion returns to the problem and summarizes the resolution. Every middle section either provides background, describes a failure, describes a solution, or gives a recommendation. If you can't write one sentence that describes a section's purpose, that section doesn't belong. Before you write a single paragraph, complete your outline. If the outline is solid, the writing will follow.

---

## Standard article structure

| Section | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| Introduction | State the problem and why it matters |
| Background | Any context the reader needs before the story |
| The failure (or challenge) | What went wrong — specific and concrete |
| The solution | What worked — how you applied it |
| Results | What changed — numbers preferred |
| Lessons learned | The generalizable insight |
| Conclusion | Return to the opening problem, summarize |

Not every article needs all 7. A 600-word article might have 5.

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read the passage.
**Minutes 6–12** — Shadow the passage.
**Minutes 13–25** — Complete the outline exercise.
**Minutes 26–30** — Read the outline aloud from start to finish. Does it flow logically?

## Anti-Translation Drill

Explain your article outline to an imaginary editor. Describe each section in one sentence. English only. 2 minutes.
