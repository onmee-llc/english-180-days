## Session goal

Choose your article topic and write one sentence that describes the article's core claim.

## Shadowing passage — choosing a topic

> When technical writers say "write what you know", they mean it literally. The best technical articles are not summaries of documentation. They are reflections on something real that happened — a problem you solved, a mistake you made, or something that worked in a surprising way. An article titled "Introduction to RAG" will compete with hundreds of similar articles. An article titled "Why Our RAG System Kept Failing and What Fixed It" is something only you can write. It has a story, a specific failure mode, and a lesson. Even a small lesson — a single configuration change that cut hallucination by 30% — is worth writing about. Readers don't need another tutorial. They need someone who has been through the same problem to tell them what actually happened. That specificity is what makes a technical article worth reading and sharing.

## Topic selection exercise

**Step 1:** List 3–5 things that happened in your KPI 1 or KPI 2 work this year that were:
- Surprising
- Harder than you expected
- Something you wish someone had written a guide about

**Step 2:** Choose the one with the most specific story. The best choice is the one where you can answer:
- What was the problem?
- What did you try that didn't work?
- What actually worked?

**Step 3:** Write one sentence — the article's core claim.
> "This article shows that [X happens] when [condition], and [specific approach] is the most reliable fix."

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read the shadowing passage silently.
**Minutes 6–12** — Shadow the passage.
**Minutes 13–22** — Do the topic selection exercise.
**Minutes 23–28** — Write your one-sentence core claim.
**Minutes 29–30** — Say the core claim aloud 3 times.

## Anti-Translation Drill

Explain your article topic to an imaginary colleague in 60 seconds. English only. Use the core claim sentence as the starting point.
