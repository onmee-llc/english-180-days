## Session goal

Write your article introduction. A strong introduction is the hardest part of a technical article. When it's done, the rest is easier.

## Shadowing passage — what a good introduction does

> A technical article introduction does three things. First, it identifies the problem with enough specificity that the reader recognizes it. Not "LLMs can sometimes produce incorrect output" — that's too general. "Our customer-facing chatbot was hallucinating pricing information, and we didn't know until a customer complained" — that is a problem with a face and a consequence. Second, the introduction signals to the reader that the writer has actually lived through this problem. Not summarized it from documentation. Actually debugged it. The reader can feel the difference. Third, it makes a promise: by the end of this article, you will have something specific you didn't have before — a technique, a decision framework, a story you can learn from. The introduction does not solve the problem. It makes the reader want to continue.

---

## Introduction structure

**Part 1 — The hook (1–2 sentences)**
A specific moment, failure, or observation that pulls the reader in.
> "Six months ago, our production LLM system was returning pricing data it had hallucinated. We didn't know until a customer called."

**Part 2 — The context (2–3 sentences)**
Enough background for the reader to understand the stakes.
> "We were running a customer service chatbot using Claude with RAG over our product database. Retrieval accuracy was 87%. That meant 13% of responses were grounded in nothing."

**Part 3 — The promise (1–2 sentences)**
What the reader will get by the end.
> "In this article, I'll show what we changed in our retrieval pipeline to push accuracy above 95%, and the three eval tests we now run before every model deployment."

## 30-minute protocol

**Minutes 1–5** — Read the passage silently.
**Minutes 6–12** — Shadow the passage.
**Minutes 13–25** — Write your 3-part introduction.
**Minutes 26–30** — Read the introduction aloud. Does it make you want to keep reading?
